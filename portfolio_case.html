OVERVIEW

Having experienced being a Freelancer in both the Education and UX Design fields, I understand the need to be a well-rounded professional who can take different roles.

But what about hired UX Designers?

From quick browsing in Forums, I noticed that the vast majority of UX designers that replied, stated they have basic coding skills. That pushed me to learn practical coding skills, allowing me to communicate efficiently with developers.

Project

Portfolio website

Role

UX / UI Designer, Developer

Tools

Figma, VS Code, GitHub

Time

9 weeks

PROBLEM:

Although I took a crash course in Java, it is very different then front-end languages which I had no knowledge of. On top of that. The Career Foundry course is mostly text based, which makes it harder to learn then video, for my taste.

SOLUTION:

To accompany my Career Foundry task-oriented course I took other deep-dive HTML, CSS, and JavaScript courses. This allowed me to build practical skills, to have greater creative freedom and to possibly cooperate better with developers. To bring these skills to light I programmed this website myself.

PROBLEM STATEMENT:

Tomer Rozenberg needs a professional portfolio website that will communicate his persona, works, skills, and ways of communication to potential recruiters and enthusiasts.

RESEARCH

Every good research should start with competitors' analysis. With zero knowledge of portfolio practices, I searched the web for common patterns and ideas. I found that most hero sections will use one of three layouts:

The Split Screen

Usually divided into thirds or halves

The Complex Layout

Usually combine different splits to create a whole

The Centered

Usually placing a title and a call-for-action button in the center

Initially, I chose to go with a Split Screen hero page, since these were the most common, simple, and intuitive for me. So I went to the drawing board and started the next phase.

IDEATE

I started to sketch the design I had in mind using Figma

PROTOTYPE

I adopted a mobile-first approach in my work. I began with fundamental HTML and progressively integrated CSS, taking a deep dive into semantic code writing and adhering to the best practices commonly used today. I came up with a color palette and a font that will give a soft, empathetic, and dreamy impression.

And this was the result

RE-RESEARCH

At this point, I wasn't happy with my design because, although represented me quite well, it felt very immature. It was essentially a first sketch to keep in the drawer and go and give it another try.
I went back to search for more inspiring portfolios, this is what I found:

JASON'S

I liked how he used an organized layout pattern to create a look that is both chaotic and organized. Together with the choice of images, it shouts “Creativity”, which is exactly what you want as a photographer. It's also worth noting that JASON'S home page has Call-for-actions buttons all around. PROJECTS on top, HIRE ME on the hero section, and many more. I will want to implement similar ideas in my portfolio.

Charlotte

Charlotte Hero's page uses the split screen layout, which I'm not fond of for portfolios, I find it more suitable for a service or product. But her choice of colors is elegant yet young and daring. It's very original.

Brono Erdison

Bruno's portfolio is so clean and elegant. It projects professionalism, expertise, and trust. I love the layout, placing the designer in the center. I also liked how he used the credentials, data, and a summary in his Hero section.

RIKU AMIN

This is perhaps my favorite, it integrates the designer in the center layout with more creative elements such as text over the hero's photo, and sketches-like images and stickers.

IDEATE

I started sketching another version that would look clean and professional. As with most projects, I had a deadline for submitting the design, so I decided to start prototyping the new version and deal with the nitty-gritty details later on

PROTOTYPE

With a clear vision of my goal, I coded a basic layout of the new design. Throughout this journey, I continuously iterated and refined, tweaking and enhancing elements that were initially overlooked or didn't align perfectly with the overall flow.
This was the result I could already test:

TEST

As a UX Designer, I know the value of good testing. I chose a variety of different users to gain as many interpretations of what people see on my website.

1. I set these goals for the testing:

1st Goal

LEARN ABOUT ME

Through my biography, skills, resume, work, and the website's general impression

2nd Goal

SEE MY WORK

Understand my work process, line of thought and problem solving skills

3rd Goal

CONTACT ME

Find an easy way to reach out for hiring

2. I crafted detailed guidelines:

1st Step

WELCOME

Ice breaking and introduction, who am I and what I do

2nd Step

INSTRUCTIONS

This is a test for the website, not for you. You cannot go wrong here. Please think out loud and express your impressions

3rd Step

SCENARIOS

Here I ask the participant to IMAGINE they are a recruiter and guide them through a flow of tasks based on the user goals

4th Step

OPEN-ENDED QUESTIONS

By the end of every scenario, I follow up with investigative questions regarding the process and the user expectations and experience

FINDINGS

I summarized my finding using an affnity map, first noting each participant on their own, before categorizing the data. The task completion rate was 100%.

CONCLUSIONS

Challenges:

The additional courses I took separately didn't always align with the CF course instruction and I had to learn by experimenting with parts of CSS and JavaScript codes. There is a very clear challenge regarding front-end development in the form of unexpected problems. Every time we make a change to the codes, position, or style of an element, it might aect another element abruptly. The ever-changing screen size of the viewer also means that it's not enough to use traditional screen sizes as breakpoints and there is a need to add breakpoints everywhere our design pattern breaks or is inaccurate.
